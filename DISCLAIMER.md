# A Note on Estimation

Estimation is one of the hardest problems in software engineering. It has
been studied for decades, and even the best methods produce ranges, not
certainties.

## The Hard Truth

- **Large migrations and rewrites can take years.** A database migration
  for a small app might take a few days; the same category of work for a
  large enterprise with legacy systems, compliance requirements, and
  distributed teams can take months or years. Complexity is not linear —
  it compounds.

- **Small tasks can be nearly instant.** With modern AI coding tools,
  well-scoped tasks that once took hours can now be completed in minutes.
  A skilled developer with a capable agent can ship a simple feature
  faster than it takes to write the ticket. The lower end of estimation
  is being compressed dramatically.

- **The gap between best and worst case is wider than you think.** The
  Cone of Uncertainty tells us that early estimates can be off by 4x in
  either direction. A task estimated at "2 weeks" might take 3 days or
  2 months depending on what you discover along the way.

## What This Skill Does Not Do

This skill provides structured, research-backed estimates — not
predictions. It cannot account for:

- Organizational politics, approval processes, or blocked calendars
- Undiscovered technical debt or hidden coupling
- External vendor delays or API changes
- Team morale, burnout, or turnover
- The difference between "works on my machine" and "works in production"
- Regulatory or compliance requirements specific to your industry

## What This Skill Does Do

- Gives you a **framework** for thinking about effort systematically
- Separates **expected** from **committed** estimates so you can plan and
  promise at different confidence levels
- Accounts for **agent effectiveness decay** — AI tools are great at
  small tasks and less reliable at large ones
- Encourages **calibration** — comparing estimates to actuals and
  improving over time
- Produces **consistent, traceable** estimates where every number can be
  traced back to a formula input

## The Best Estimate Is a Calibrated One

Research shows that estimation accuracy improves from 64% to 81% with
feedback loops. The most important thing you can do is:

1. Estimate before you start
2. Track how long it actually took
3. Compare and adjust your ratios
4. Repeat

No formula will ever replace this cycle. This skill gives you the
structure; your data makes it accurate.
